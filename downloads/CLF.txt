Abstract
We propose CLF-Guided Training, a principled framework that reformulates momentum SGD as a controlled dynamical system. By constructing a composite Lyapunov function integrating loss regret (potential energy), momentum magnitude (kinetic energy), and directional curvature (sharpness energy), we enforce an Input-to-State Stability (ISS) constraint to derive an explicit, adaptive gradient‑scaling rule. The rule provably balances stability and precision via the interpretable parameter
δ
δ, avoids Hessian computation via a first‑order finite‑difference curvature estimator, and yields non‑asymptotic convergence rates with explicit dependence on algorithmic parameters. Theoretical analysis establishes convergence in non‑convex and PL settings, while practical considerations ensure minimal overhead.

1. Introduction and Related Work
The interpretation of optimization algorithms as dynamical systems enables stability analysis via control‑theoretic tools. Polyak’s heavy‑ball method (1964) pioneered Lyapunov‑based analysis for momentum optimization. Subsequent work extended this to convex (Su et al., 2016) and non‑convex settings (Li et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2020) using ODE approximations. Recent advances employ integral quadratic constraints (Lessard et al., 2016) and contraction analysis (Fazlyab et al., 2020) for robust analysis of adaptive methods.

Control‑theoretic approaches to adaptive optimization leverage input‑to‑state stability (ISS), e.g., adaptive gradient clipping (Cutkosky & Orabona, 2021) and ISS‑inspired stepsize rules (Borkar, 2008). Parallelly, neural Lyapunov learning certifies stability in control (Richards et al., 2018; Berkenkamp et al., 2021) but focuses on verification, not design.

Our work synthesizes these directions by:
(i) Constructing a composite Lyapunov function that explicitly incorporates loss‑landscape curvature via a bias‑free sharpness estimate,
(ii) Enforcing an ISS constraint to derive a provably safe gradient‑scaling rule,
(iii) Demonstrating explicit convergence rates with tunable stability‑precision trade‑offs.
Unlike prior Lyapunov‑based optimizers focusing solely on potential or kinetic energy, we treat optimization as a second‑order mechanical system where total energy must remain within a stability certificate. Crucially, we estimate curvature via first‑order finite differences, avoiding Hessian computations and enabling scalability to deep networks.

5. Algorithm (CLF‑Guided Training)
The theoretical derivations above translate directly into the following practical procedure.

Algorithm 1 CLF‑Guided Training (one iteration)

Input: current parameters θ_t, momentum m_t,
hyper‑parameters β, η, s_min, α_V, γ, δ, λ_0, ε, K, X,
state variables: Ȳ_K_t, Ȳ_ρ_t, Δ_stale, Φ_t (or its approximation)

1. Sample mini‑batch ξ_t and compute stochastic gradient
g_t = ∇ℓ(θ_t; ξ_t)

2. (Every K steps or if ‖g_t‖ ↑ > X% over 3 steps)
• Sample an independent mini‑batch ξ_t^+
• Compute perturbed gradient
g_t^+ = ∇ℓ(θ_t + ε·ĝ_t; ξ_t^+) where ĝ_t = g_t / ‖g_t‖
• Compute curvature estimate
ρ_t = (ĝ_tᵀ g_t^+ − ĝ_tᵀ g_t) / ε
• Update EMA of curvature:
Ȳ_ρ_t = λ_0 Ȳ_ρ_{t-1} + (1−λ_0) ρ_t
• Update Δ_stale (if using the K‑step schedule):

10. Conclusion
We have presented CLF‑Guided Training, a theoretically grounded framework that treats momentum SGD as a controlled dynamical system and uses ISS to derive a provably safe, curvature‑aware gradient‑scaling rule. Our method improves upon prior Lyapunov‑based optimizers by incorporating curvature without Hessian computations, avoids the distributional assumptions of ODE/SDE approaches, and provides finer‑grained control than norm‑only ISS methods. The theory establishes a foundation for adaptive optimization where stability is not heuristic but enforced by a verifiable energy certificate—paving the way for principled, scalable tuning of deep‑learning optimizers.
